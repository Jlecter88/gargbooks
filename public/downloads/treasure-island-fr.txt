# *Treasure Island*

**PART ONE—The Old Buccaneer**

***

## I. The Old Sea-dog at the Admiral Benbow . . . . 11

Squire Trelawney, Dr. Livesey, and the rest of these gentlemen having asked me to set down all the particulars concerning Treasure Island, from its very beginning unto its end—keeping nothing back save the bearings of the island, and that only because there remains still treasure yet unlifted—I take up my pen in the year of grace 17—, and return to the time when my father kept the Admiral Benbow inn, and the brown old seaman with the sabre cut first took his lodging under our roof.

I remember him as if it were but yesterday, as he came trudging toward the inn door, his sea-chest following behind him in a hand-barrow—a tall, stout, heavy man, of nut-brown complexion; his tarry pigtail fell over the shoulder of his soiled blue coat; his hands were ragged and scarred, with black, broken nails, and the sabre cut across one cheek, a dirty, livid white. I recall him looking around the cove and whistling to himself as he did so, and then breaking into that old sea-song which he sang so often afterwards:

> “Fifteen men on the dead man’s chest—
> Yo-ho-ho, and a bottle of rum!”

in the high, aged voice that seemed tuned and broken upon the capstan bars. Then he rapped upon the door with a bit of stick, like a handspike that he carried, and when my father appeared, called roughly for a glass of rum. This, when brought to him, he drank slowly, like a connoisseur, lingering upon the taste whilst still looking about at the cliffs and up at our signboard.

“This is a handy cove,” says he at last; “and a pleasant sittyated grog-shop. Much company, mate?”

My father told him no, very little company, the more was the pity.

“Well, then,” said he, “this is the berth for me. Here you, matey,” he cried to the man who trundled the barrow; “bring up alongside and help up my chest. I’ll stay here a bit,” he continued. “I am a plain man; rum and bacon and eggs is what I want, and that head up there for to watch ships off. What you mought call me? You mought call me captain. Oh, I see what you’re at—there”; and he threw down three or four gold pieces upon the threshold. “You can tell me when I’ve worked through that,” says he, looking as fierce as a commander.

And indeed, though poor were his clothes and coarse his speech, he possessed none of the appearance of a man who sailed before the mast; rather, he resembled a mate or skipper accustomed to being obeyed or to striking. The man who accompanied him with the barrow told us that the mail had set him down the morning prior at the Royal George, that he had inquired after inns along the coast, and hearing ours well spoken of—I suppose, and described as lonely—he had chosen it from the others for his place of residence. And that was all we could learn of our guest.

He was a very silent man by custom. All day he lingered around the cove or upon the cliffs with a brass telescope; all evening he sat in a corner of the parlour next to the fire and drank rum and water very strong. Mostly, he would not speak when spoken to, but only look up—sudden and fierce—and blow through his nose like a fog-horn; and we, along with the people who frequented our house, soon learned to let him be. Every day, upon returning from his stroll, he would inquire if any seafaring men had passed by along the road. At first, we thought it was lack of company of his own kind that made him ask this question, but at last, we began to see he was desirous to *avoid* them. When a seaman did stop at the Admiral Benbow (as now and then some did, making for Bristol by the coast road), he would peer into him through the curtained door before entering the parlour; and he was always sure to be as silent as a mouse when any such was present. For me, at least, there was no secret about the matter, for I was, in a way, a sharer in his alarms. He had taken me aside one day and promised me a silver fourpenny on the first of every month if I would only keep my “weather-eye open for a seafaring man with one leg” and let him know the moment he appeared. Often enough when the first of the month arrived and I applied to him for my wages, he would merely blow through his nose at me and stare me down, but before the week was out, he was sure to think better of it, bring me my four-penny piece, and repeat his orders to watch for “the seafaring man with one leg.”

How that personage haunted my dreams, I need scarcely tell you. On stormy nights, when the wind shook the four corners of the house and the surf roared along the cove up the cliffs, I would see him in a thousand forms, and with a thousand diabolical expressions. Now the leg would be cut off at the knee, now at the hip; now he was a monstrous sort of creature who had never possessed but that one leg, and it situated right in the middle of his body. To see him leap and run and pursue me over hedge and ditch was the worst of nightmares. And altogether I paid quite dearly for my monthly fourpenny piece, in the shape of these abominable fancies.

But though I was so terrified by the notion of the seafaring man with one leg, I was far less afraid of the captain himself than any other who knew him. There were nights when he consumed a deal more rum and water than his head could carry; and then he would sometimes sit and sing his wicked, old, wild sea-songs, minding nobody; but at other times he would call for glasses round and force all the trembling company to listen to his tales or bear chorus to his singing. Often I have heard the house shaking with “Yo-ho-ho, and a bottle of rum,” all the neighbours joining in for dear life, with the fear of death upon them, and each singing louder than the other to avoid remark. For in these fits he was the most overriding companion ever known; he would slap his hand on the table for silence all around; he would fly up in a passion of anger at a question, or sometimes because none was posed, and thus judged the company was not following his narrative. Nor would he allow anyone to leave the inn till he had drunk himself sleepy and reeled off to bed.
